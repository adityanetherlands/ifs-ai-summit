# Future of M&A

**Subtitle:** Rethinking the Scout-Diligence-Integrate Pipeline with AI

---

## 1. The Current State

Every M&A function runs on the same lifecycle: scout, then diligence, then integrate. The Scout-Diligence-Integrate process is the operational backbone. And right now, it is broken at every stage.

**Scout is inefficient.** Target screening is manual: analysts research companies one by one from databases and news.

**Diligence is structurally neglected.** Legal contract review (customer, partner, employment) takes 4–8 weeks.

**Integrate is reactive.** Integration planning starts after close – not during diligence – leading to surprises.

### Key Statistics

| Metric | Value | Detail |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Due Diligence Duration | 3–6 months | Average for mid-market enterprise software acquisitions |
| Data Room Documents | 5,000–15,000 | Typical document count in an enterprise software VDR |
| Deal Success Rate | 50–60% | Of M&A deals that achieve stated synergies within 3 years |
| Integration Overrun | 25–40% | Of integration budgets exceeded |

### Traditional vs AI-Native

**Traditional Scout-Diligence-Integrate:**
- Target screening is manual: analysts research companies one by one from databases and news
- Financial modelling takes 2–4 weeks per target for a basic DCF and synergy model
- Due diligence data rooms of 10,000+ documents reviewed manually by teams of lawyers and analysts
- Legal contract review (customer, partner, employment) takes 4–8 weeks
- Integration planning starts after close – not during diligence – leading to surprises
- Post-merger integration tracked via spreadsheets and monthly check-ins

**AI-Native Scout-Diligence-Integrate:**
- AI continuously screens 10,000+ targets against IFS's strategic criteria – flagging fits automatically
- Financial models auto-generated in hours: DCF, synergy, accretion/dilution, scenario analysis
- AI reads and analyses entire data rooms in days: flagging risks, summarising contracts, identifying issues
- Legal review of 1,000+ contracts in 48 hours – AI extracts key terms, flags unusual clauses
- Integration planning starts during diligence: AI generates the integration blueprint from discovered data
- Post-merger tracked automatically: AI monitors integration milestones, flags delays, measures synergies

## 2. How to Think About the Future of M&A

The Scout-Diligence-Integrate lifecycle forces specificity. Not "AI will transform M&A" but exactly how each stage changes when AI agents handle the operational layer end-to-end.

### The Scout Stage: From Opportunistic to Systematic Targeting

IFS has a clear M&A thesis: expand the industrial software portfolio (the Softeon warehouse management acquisition demonstrates the strategy). Today, target identification relies on banker relationships, conference networking, and analyst research. AI transforms scouting: platforms like DealRoom, PitchBook, and Grata continuously screen thousands of companies against IFS's criteria – product fit, technology stack, customer overlap, financial health, and cultural compatibility. AI monitors trigger events: a target raises pricing, loses a key customer, or its founders update their LinkedIn to 'open to opportunities.' The corp dev team stops reacting to banker decks and starts driving a systematic pipeline.

### The Diligence Stage: From Document Marathons to AI-Assisted Analysis

Due diligence at IFS's scale (acquiring enterprise software companies) means reviewing thousands of documents: customer contracts, employment agreements, IP filings, financial statements, and technical architecture docs. AI compresses this from months to weeks. Legal AI (Harvey, Kira/Litera) reads contracts and extracts key terms, change-of-control clauses, and risk provisions. Financial AI generates valuation models from uploaded financials. Technical AI assesses code quality, architecture compatibility, and technical debt. The human team focuses on judgement: strategic fit assessment, cultural evaluation, and negotiation strategy.

### The Integrate Stage: From Post-Close Chaos to Pre-Planned Execution

Most M&A integration failures happen because planning starts too late. AI enables 'integration by design': during diligence, AI generates the integration blueprint – systems migration plan, organisational structure options, customer communication templates, and synergy tracking framework. Post-close, AI monitors integration milestones, tracks synergy realisation, and flags risks before they escalate. For IFS, with its productisation mandate (acquired capabilities should integrate into IFS Cloud), AI can even model the technical integration pathway during diligence.

### Scout-Diligence-Integrate as a Continuous Loop

In the AI-native model, every completed acquisition enriches the targeting model (what worked, what didn't). Every integration generates lessons that improve the next diligence process. The M&A function becomes a learning machine: each deal makes the next one faster, cheaper, and more likely to succeed. For IFS, with EQT's M&A-friendly ownership structure and a stated growth strategy, this flywheel is a structural advantage.

### North Star Target

> **From ~15–20 (Corp Dev, Legal M&A, Integration PMO, Financial Analysts) → 8–12 people**
> 
> Deals evaluated per analyst per quarter: from 3–5 to 15–20
> 
> AI automates 70% of target screening, financial modelling, and due diligence data room analysis. Legal document review (contracts, IP, compliance) shifts from weeks of lawyer time to hours of AI analysis with human review. Integration planning becomes template-driven. The team stays small but processes 3–4x more targets at higher quality.

### Target KPIs

| KPI | Target | Context |
|-----|--------|---------|
| Due Diligence Duration | 4–6 weeks | Down from 3–6 months |
| Targets Screened | 200+/quarter | Up from 10–20 manually |
| M&A FTE Count | 8–12 | Down from ~15–20 at IFS scale |
| Data Room Analysis | Days | Down from weeks for 10,000+ documents |
| Integration Success | >80% | Synergies achieved within 2 years |
| Contract Review Time | 48 hours | For 1,000+ contracts (down from 4–8 weeks) |

## 3. Challenging Questions for the Room

These are not abstract thought experiments. They are the outcomes your organisation needs to engineer in the next 12 to 24 months.

### Scout
- How do we achieve continuous, AI-powered target screening – where the system evaluates 10,000+ companies against IFS's strategic criteria and flags the top 20 fits weekly – without any analyst manually researching companies?
- What has to be true for IFS's M&A pipeline to be as systematic and data-driven as its sales pipeline – with AI-scored targets, stage gates, and predicted close probability?

### Diligence
- How do we compress due diligence from 3–6 months to 4–6 weeks – where AI reads the entire data room, flags every risk, and generates a preliminary investment memo before a single analyst opens a document?
- If AI can review 1,000 customer contracts in 48 hours and flag every change-of-control clause, unusual term, and revenue concentration risk – what does the legal diligence team actually spend their time on?

### Integrate
- How do we make integration planning start during diligence – where AI generates the systems migration plan, org design options, and synergy tracking framework from the data room before the deal even closes?
- What has to be true for every IFS acquisition to have a day-one integration blueprint that includes technical architecture compatibility, IFS Cloud migration pathway, and go-to-market integration plan – produced by AI during diligence?

### North Star
- If the same 8–12 people can evaluate 4x more targets and close deals 3x faster, does IFS's M&A cadence accelerate – and what does that mean for the growth strategy?
- How do we capture and codify M&A lessons so that each acquisition makes the next one better – and is AI the mechanism for turning institutional M&A knowledge into a reusable asset?
- If AI handles 70% of diligence and integration planning, does IFS need an in-house M&A team at all – or does a 4-person strategic team plus AI replace the traditional corporate development function?

## 5. Hands-On Automation Exercises

Do at least one before the session. Do all four if you want to arrive with a working prototype, not an opinion.

### Exercise 1: The AI Target Screener
Define IFS's ideal acquisition criteria: product adjacency (WMS, APM, IoT, vertical SaaS), revenue range, technology stack compatibility, customer overlap potential, and geographic fit. Feed these into Claude with access to public company databases. Ask it to generate a ranked list of 20 potential targets with fit scores and rationale. Compare to your current pipeline.
- **Output:** An AI-generated M&A target list with fit scores and strategic rationale – benchmarked against the current pipeline.
- **Tools:** Claude, PitchBook/Crunchbase data, web search · **Effort:** 60 min

### Exercise 2: The Due Diligence Accelerator
Take a set of 50 sample contracts (anonymised or mock). Feed them into AI and ask it to: extract key terms (duration, value, change-of-control, exclusivity, termination), flag unusual clauses, identify revenue concentration risk, and generate a summary memo. Measure time vs. manual legal review.
- **Output:** An AI-generated contract analysis summary with flagged risks and key terms – with a time comparison to manual review.
- **Tools:** Claude, sample contracts (PDF), Python · **Effort:** 90 min

### Exercise 3: The Integration Blueprint Generator
Take a hypothetical acquisition target (e.g., a mid-market FSM vendor with 200 employees, $40M ARR, and a .NET tech stack). Ask AI to generate a complete integration blueprint: systems migration plan to IFS Cloud, org design options, customer communication plan, synergy estimates, and a 100-day execution timeline.
- **Output:** A complete AI-generated integration blueprint for a hypothetical acquisition – ready for review by the integration PMO.
- **Tools:** Claude, IFS Cloud architecture docs, integration templates · **Effort:** 90 min

### Exercise 4: The Synergy Model
Build an interactive financial model for a hypothetical IFS acquisition. Inputs: target ARR, growth rate, gross margin, headcount, and IFS's cost of capital. Outputs: DCF valuation range, revenue synergy estimates (cross-sell to IFS base), cost synergy estimates (shared infrastructure, redundant roles), and accretion/dilution analysis. Make it interactive enough for a board presentation.
- **Output:** An interactive M&A valuation and synergy model that the board could use to evaluate acquisition targets.
- **Tools:** Python/Excel, Claude, HTML/ECharts · **Effort:** 2 hours

## 6. What to Research Before the Session

Four tasks to ground the discussion in your reality.

### 1. Audit Your Diligence Timeline
Map the last acquisition end-to-end: from first contact to close to integration complete. Identify the longest phases. For each phase, estimate: what percentage of elapsed time was data gathering/analysis vs. strategic judgement? The data percentage is your AI opportunity.

### 2. Review Your Target Pipeline
How many potential targets has your corp dev team evaluated in the last 12 months? How were they sourced? How many came from systematic screening vs. banker relationships or conference encounters? The systematic percentage tells you how much upside AI targeting would create.

### 3. Explore One Platform
Spend 20 minutes on Harvey or Grata. Harvey shows you the future of AI-powered legal diligence. Grata shows you the future of AI target discovery. Both are production-grade and used by PE firms and corporate dev teams.

### 4. Ask the Uncomfortable Question
Ask your head of corp dev: 'If AI screened targets continuously, analysed data rooms in days, and generated integration blueprints before close – could a team of 8 do what our team of 20 does today, but evaluate 4x more targets?' If the answer is yes, the transformation case writes itself.

> The M&A teams that create the most value will not be the ones with the most analysts. They will be the ones where AI handles the mechanics of screening, diligence, and integration planning – and the humans focus entirely on strategic judgement: which deals to do, and which to walk away from.
