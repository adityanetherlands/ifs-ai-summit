1. The Shift

Klarna halved its marketing team from 200 to 100, cut agency spend by 25%, and saved $10 million annually while increasing campaign output. AI-powered images that took six weeks now take seven days. 87% of employees use generative AI daily. The structural claim: the function operates at a different size and a different cadence – not the same team doing more.

Stripe has rewired parts of its revenue engine around AI, collapsing the distance between signal and decision. Product usage, transaction data, and merchant behaviour feed continuous intelligence into marketing and growth loops, so choices about what to offer, when to intervene, and which accounts to expand happen in near real time rather than on a quarterly planning cycle. Marketing stops being a campaign function and becomes a revenue-intelligence function.

Duolingo declared "AI-first" in 2024. The headline was content output (four to five times the previous rate), but the structural change was deeper: contractor roles eliminated, teams reorganised around AI-native product development, and a new operating rule – no feature ships without asking "Can AI eliminate this function entirely?" Revenue crossed $1 billion. The bottleneck they removed was decision-to-ship velocity, not content volume.

Three companies, three approaches, one pattern: the bottleneck in marketing was never output or creativity. It was the lag between signal and decision, and between decision and execution. AI collapses both. The organisations that move first are pulling away – not because they produce more, but because they operate at a cadence the rest of the market cannot match.

2. Your Pre-Summit Homework

Three things to bring to the session. Not to present, but to think about. Write them down. Short answers are fine. What matters is that you've actually wrestled with them.

1. A bold vision for 1 January 2027

If you fast-forwarded 8 months, what does your marketing function look like? Not incremental. Drastically different. What's disappeared? What's new? What does "a marketing team" even mean? Write the headline version: if someone wrote a profile of your function in January 2027, what would it say?

2. The case for transformation

Why does your marketing function need to transform, and why now? Not "AI is important" generically. The specific commercial, competitive, or structural reason transformation is non-negotiable for you. What breaks if you stay as you are? If you can't articulate this in two sentences, your team won't follow you.

3. The KPIs that matter in the new era

What are the 2-3 metrics that tell you marketing is winning in an AI-native world? Insight-to-decision latency? Brief-to-publish time? Percentage of campaigns running autonomously? Attribution accuracy? Pipeline influenced per marketer? The old KPIs of output, reach, and impressions measure effort. What measures outcome in a world where effort is automated?

3. Directional Nudges: Sense - Decide - Execute

Sense: From Quarterly Reports to Continuous Market Intelligence

What if your marketing intelligence was continuous, autonomous, and more current than any analyst could produce, and no human touched the data?

Platforms like 6sense and Demandbase already monitor intent signals across the web, identifying in-market accounts before they raise their hand. Attribution platforms automatically connect every touchpoint (email, ad, event, content) to pipeline and revenue in real time, with consent-aware data handling baked into the pipeline rather than bolted on. Customer feedback platforms synthesise support tickets, reviews, and social mentions into market-level insights within hours.

The structural change: sensing shifts from a team of analysts preparing decks to an always-on intelligence layer. Marketers shift from compiling reports to interrogating data. The question changes from "What happened last quarter?" to "What should we do about what's happening right now?"

Decide: From Campaign Calendars to Dynamic Optimisation

What if every campaign decision (channel mix, budget allocation, messaging, creative variant) was informed by real-time performance data and AI-modelled scenarios before launch?

Platforms like Mutiny and Demandbase autonomously test creative variants, channel mixes, and audience segments, running dozens of experiments in parallel. Claude and similar reasoning models synthesise competitive reports, audience research, and campaign performance data into strategic recommendations in minutes. Marketing automation platforms now include AI-powered next-best-action engines that recommend which channel, message, and offer will resonate most with each prospect – and dynamically reallocate budget across touchpoints as performance data arrives.

The structural change: campaign optimisation becomes algorithmic instead of manual. The "decision meeting" transforms: instead of debating what to test, teams debate what the data is telling them and why the AI recommendation might miss something human judgement catches.

Execute: From Brief-to-Ship Handoffs to Autonomous Orchestration

What if every brief became a fully orchestrated plan within an hour – work routed across teams, tools, and channels automatically, AI tracking progress, reassembling outputs, and publishing without a human project manager in the loop?

The orchestration layer is already in production. MCP servers expose workflow tools (Asana, Jira, your CMS, your marketing automation) as callable surfaces for AI. Agent platforms take a one-page brief and produce a plan, a timeline, asset drafts, and publishing schedules in parallel. Publishing platforms with AI-assisted components shrink production cycles from weeks to hours. Translation and personalisation layers localise content for every market and audience segment without a human touching the CMS.

The structural change: the marketing organisation stops being a production line with handoffs and becomes a decision-to-execution loop. The roles that survive are strategists, builders, and storytellers. The roles that disappear first are the coordinators, the CMS operators, and the brief-to-plan translators in between.

The Loop Closes

In the AI-native marketing function, insight is continuous, decisions are rapid, and execution is autonomous. The marketing calendar dissolves into a continuous optimisation loop. The CMO's job shifts from "Did we hit our number?" to "Are we capturing every signal and acting on it instantly – and is the function small enough and fast enough to do it at the cadence the market now demands?"

Thought Exercise

How would you rebuild your marketing function at less than half its current footprint – and have it outperform today's? Not through layoffs. Through eliminating the work AI makes redundant: the coordinators, the list-cleaners, the CMS operators, the brief-to-plan translators, the post-event content packagers, the manual attribution analysts. What survives is strategy, judgement, and storytelling. What new roles do you hire that nobody on your org chart today is doing?

The Role You Haven’t Hired Yet

In an AI-native marketing function, the scarcest and most valuable hire is the builder – someone inside marketing, not in IT, who can stand up a workflow, ship a prototype, and deploy AI capability in days, not quarters. Call them Marketing Engineers.

The provocation: what if every marketing pod had an embedded builder? What if half your 2026 marketing hires were technical? What if the growth engine shifted from "brief → agency → output" to "idea → builder → live in 48 hours"? Marketing functions that make this hiring shift in 2026 will operate on a different cadence than those that do not.

5. Hands-On Exercises

Do at least one before the session. These are designed to give you first-hand experience of what AI-native marketing feels like - not just an opinion about it.

Exercise 1: The Brief-to-Plan Orchestrator

30 min

Take a real campaign brief from the last quarter. Use Claude (or equivalent) with your workflow tool (Asana, Jira, Notion). In one session, produce: a task breakdown with owners, a day-by-day timeline, an asset list mapped to channels, and a publishing sequence. Measure elapsed time from brief to fully-planned campaign. Compare to your current brief-to-plan handoff – how much of the gap is tooling versus process?

Output: A fully planned campaign from a one-page brief, in under 30 minutes.
Tools: Claude, a real brief, your workflow tool

Exercise 2: The Intent-to-Pipeline Dashboard

60 min

Export data from your marketing automation and CRM. Build an interactive dashboard showing: campaign ROI by source, conversion velocity by stage, and AI-recommended next actions per account. The goal is to see what an always-on marketing intelligence layer looks like.

Output: An interactive attribution dashboard connecting spend to pipeline with AI insights.
Tools: Claude, Python or Excel, CRM/marketing data exports

Exercise 3: The Figma-to-Production Sprint

60 min

Pick a single landing-page component. With Cursor (or equivalent), a Figma design, and access to your CMS, produce a working, deployable component end-to-end. Measure: did it ship? Where did the blockers appear? Compare to your current design-to-deploy cycle and estimate how much of the gap is tooling versus process, governance, or handoff friction.

Output: A deployable component in under 60 minutes.
Tools: Cursor, Figma, access to your CMS

Exercise 4: The ABM Signal-to-Send Sprint

60 min

Pick one target account. Pull intent data from your platform (6sense, Demandbase, or LinkedIn Sales Navigator signals). Use Claude to synthesise a behavioural summary, a personalised outreach message referencing specific signals, and a 3-touch sequence tailored to the account’s likely buying stage. Compare: what would your normal outbound cadence look like for this account, and how much faster – and more relevant – is this?

Output: A fully personalised ABM play for a real target account in 60 minutes.
Tools: Claude, intent data, your CRM or engagement platform
