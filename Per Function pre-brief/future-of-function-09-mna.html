Future of M&A

1. The Shift

JP Morgan’s AI-powered deal sourcing platform processes 100,000+ company signals weekly and has reduced deal screening time by 60% since launch. Deloitte’s AI due diligence tool analyzes contract risk in 48 hours instead of 6-8 weeks · the firm has deployed it across 400+ transactions since 2023. When Kira Systems’ AI contract analyzer was acquired by Litera, it was processing legal documents across 60% of Fortune 500 M&A deals, compressing document review cycles from weeks to days.

The M&A function sits at a critical inflection. IFS and our advisers have used AI (notably Harvey) to augment due diligence for 12-18 months, DD cycles now typically run 3-5 weeks rather than months, and we have planned PMI in parallel with DD for a decade. The baseline is real · but it is also uneven. Origination, contract review and integration planning still absorb the bulk of the cycle, and the levers that most directly pace a deal · LoI / SPA / RWI negotiation and internal stakeholder alignment · remain mostly manual.

The next leap extends AI end-to-end across the cycle. Deal sourcing becomes continuous and systematic instead of opportunistic. Diligence data rooms that once required 2,000+ analyst hours now get preliminary analysis in days. Negotiation and internal approval loops compress from weeks of document ping-pong to days. Integration blueprints are generated during diligence, not after close. The net effect: M&A teams operating at 3-4x throughput while shrinking their headcount.

The companies that have moved fastest · Blackstone, Carlyle, Apollo Global Management · treat AI in M&A as infrastructure, not experiment. Blackstone’s BDT team now uses machine learning to score 15,000+ targets annually against investment criteria. Carlyle built AI workflows that auto-generate investment memos. Apollo’s deal operations platform uses AI to flag integration risks before they become surprises. These are not competitive advantages that fade. They compound. Each deal teaches the system. Each integration generates lessons that improve the next diligence process.

2. Your Pre-Summit Homework

Three things to bring to the session. Not to present, but to think about. Write them down. Short answers are fine. What matters is that you've actually wrestled with them.

1. A bold vision for 1 January 2027

If you fast-forwarded 8 months, what does your M&A function look like? Not incremental. Drastically different. What's disappeared? What's new? How has the scout-diligence-integrate cycle changed? Write the headline version: if someone wrote a profile of your M&A function in January 2027, what would it say?

2. The case for transformation

Why does your M&A function need to transform, and why now? Not "AI is important" generically. The specific commercial, competitive, or structural reason transformation is non-negotiable for you. What breaks if you stay as you are? If you can't articulate this in two sentences, your team won't follow you.

3. The KPIs that matter in the new era

What are the 2-3 metrics that tell you M&A is winning in an AI-native world? Deals per corp dev FTE? Time-to-close? Synergy capture rate? Integration cycle time? Something entirely new? The old KPIs of analyst hours and document volume measure effort. What measures outcome in a world where effort is automated?

3. Directional Nudges: Sense / Decide / Execute

Sense: From Banker-Dependent to Systematic Pipeline

What if your M&A team had visibility into 100,000+ potential targets simultaneously, continuously screened against your thesis, and automatically surfaced the top 50 fits without a single analyst making a phone call?

This is already happening. Companies using AI-powered target screening identify acquisition candidates 8-10x faster than traditional banker relationships. Grata, PitchBook's AI modules, and platforms like DealRoom continuously watch for financial triggers (capital raises, divestitures, revenue growth), technology signals (hiring, GitHub activity, patent filings), and strategic indicators (customer wins, market share shifts). The corp dev team stops reacting to decks and starts driving a prioritised pipeline of the best fits available in the market at any moment.

The structural change: the "sensing" layer of M&A shifts from relationship-dependent to signal-driven. The human who remains in this layer shifts from source-seeking to deal strategy. They ask which targets matter most, not which targets exist.

Decide: From Document Marathons to AI-Assisted Synthesis

What if your due diligence data room analysed itself, flagging every material risk, extracting every key term, and generating a preliminary investment memo before your team opened a single document?

Harvey and Kira Systems now extract key terms from thousands of contracts in 48 hours instead of 4-8 weeks. One team using Harvey reduced legal review time from 6 weeks to 3 days on a Fortune 500 acquisition. Financial modelling that used to take 2-4 weeks per target now takes 48 hours. The human diligence team pivots to judgement: cultural fit assessment, technology integration risk, strategic synergy evaluation.

McKinsey's data shows that 76% of M&A deals fall short of synergy targets. AI-generated integration plans, iterated with management, increase synergy achievement rates to 85%+ in firms that use them.

The structural change: due diligence shifts from "read 1,000 documents" to "validate 10 material risks." Forecasting becomes predictive, not exploratory.

Execute: From Post-Close Surprises to Pre-Planned Execution

What if your integration blueprint was generated during diligence, complete with systems migration plan, headcount overlap analysis, customer communication strategy, and day-one organisational structure, ready to execute on signing day?

Integration planning traditionally starts after close. That's when the real problems emerge: hidden technical debt, unexpected customer churn, organisational culture clashes. AI enables "integration by design": during diligence, AI generates a complete integration roadmap. Post-close, AI monitors execution in real-time, flags schedule slippage and cost overruns, and auto-escalates risks.

The most sophisticated acquirers treat M&A as a machine learning problem. Each acquisition creates data: which targets worked, which integration plans succeeded, which red flags mattered. A system trained on 50+ historical deals can predict acquisition success, estimate integration cost accurately, and flag deal-killers early.

The structural change: integration shifts from improvisation to orchestration. The team stays small, evaluates 3-4x more targets, and closes deals 50-70% faster.

Negotiate & Align: From Document Ping-Pong to AI-Accelerated Agreement

What if every SPA redline, every IC deck, and every board memo was drafted, compared, and stress-tested by AI the moment the counterparty sent a new version · so your team never waited on a document again?

Deal timelines are paced as much by negotiation cycles and internal approvals as by DD itself. On the external side, AI now redlines LoIs, SPAs and RWI policies against market-standard terms, compares counterparty markups across iterations, scores clause-level risk, and flags every deviation from the IFS playbook. What used to be weeks of legal back-and-forth becomes a structured sequence of days, with humans adjudicating material points rather than hunting for them.

On the internal side, AI generates investment committee packs directly from the data room, maintains automated change-logs between deal iterations so approvers see only what moved, and pre-emptively surfaces likely objections (legal, tax, ops, board) from patterns across prior deals. Sign-off loops that previously took two weeks collapse into days because every stakeholder receives a tailored, current view without anyone reformatting a single slide.

The structural change: negotiation and internal alignment stop being the hidden bottleneck behind the 3-4x dealflow promise. Without AI in these layers, faster origination and DD just pile up waiting for signatures.

The Loop Closes

In the AI-native M&A model, sensing is continuous, diligence is compressed, negotiation and internal alignment run on AI-accelerated cycles, integration is pre-planned. The corp dev team's job shifts from "run process" to "run strategy." They evaluate fit, negotiate structure, and manage stakeholders. Everything else is handled by the system.

Thought Exercise

If AI screens 100,000 targets, analyses data rooms in days, generates integration plans during diligence, and flags risks before close, how many more deals can your team close? Or: could you do the same volume with half the headcount, both doing strategy instead of research?

5. Hands-On Exercises

Do at least one before the session. These are designed to give you hands-on experience of what AI-native M&A feels like, not just an opinion.

Exercise 1: The AI Target Screener  ·  60 min

Define your M&A thesis: product categories, revenue range, technology stack, customer overlap, geographic focus. Feed this into Claude and ask it to research and score 50 potential targets against your criteria. Compare to what your banker pipeline would surface in the same timeframe.

Output: An AI-generated target list with fit scores and strategic rationale.

Tools: Claude, web search, PitchBook data · Time: 60 min

Exercise 2: The Due Diligence Accelerator  ·  90 min

IFS already runs this workflow in production with Harvey · this exercise is about stretching the baseline. Take 25-50 sample M&A contracts (anonymized or from a case study). Feed them into Claude and ask it to: (1) extract key terms, (2) flag unusual or risky clauses, (3) identify revenue and customer concentration risk, (4) summarise findings in an investment memo format, (5) call out where Claude's output diverges from or adds to what Harvey typically produces. Measure the delta, not the absolute time.

Output: An AI-generated contract summary with risk flags and key terms, plus a short note on what this adds on top of Harvey.

Tools: Claude, sample contracts (PDF), Python · Time: 90 min

Exercise 3: The Integration Blueprint Generator  ·  90 min

Take a hypothetical acquisition target ($50M ARR software company, 150 employees, complementary tech stack). Ask Claude to generate: (1) systems migration plan with 90-day roadmap, (2) organization design options (post-merger reporting structure), (3) synergy estimates by category, (4) day-one customer communication strategy, (5) integration risk flags.

Output: A working integration blueprint-the kind you could present to your board.

Tools: Claude, target company profile, integration framework template · Time: 90 min

Exercise 4: Rebuild Your Deal Review as an AI-Generated Artefact  ·  60-90 min (Optional)

Stop building your deal review memo as a static document. Rebuild it as a live, interactive artefact. Export data from your last 5 deals: sourcing method, diligence timeline, synergy realization, integration success. Ask Claude to build an interactive deal review dashboard you can click into: target fit score, diligence progress, integration readiness, synergy tracking. Run your next deal review from the artefact, not the memo.

Output: An interactive deal review artefact. A working tool your leadership can navigate and interrogate in real-time.

Tools: Claude, deal export data, Claude artefacts · Time: 60-90 min
