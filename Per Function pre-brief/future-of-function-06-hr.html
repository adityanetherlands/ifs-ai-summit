Future of HR

What happens when AI collapses friction across the entire talent lifecycle?

1. The Shift

Unilever screened 250,000 applications, hired 800 candidates, and reclaimed 50,000 hours of recruiter and candidate time. More than £1 million in annual savings. The time-to-hire dropped 75-90%. What changed? Not the number of recruiters. The structure of recruiting itself. Where screening used to consume weeks, AI now handles it in days. Where scheduling took manual work, conversational AI agents do it in minutes. Where assessments were gut-based, neuroscience games now predict cognitive fit with precision.

LinkedIn reports that companies leading on internal mobility see 53% longer employee tenures. But internal moves are administratively brutal · spanning employment contracts, cost centres, payroll, systems provisioning, benefits, and reporting lines. Gloat and Eightfold have made internal moves transparent and seamless, turning a four-week friction point into a capability unlock. Employees see opportunities in real time, match their skills, and move in days instead of months.

IBM built a predictive attrition model that flags flight risk with 95% accuracy · 3-6 months ahead. When you can predict departures, you stop reacting and start intervening. Personalized retention plans. Opportunity matching. Compensation adjustments. Institutional knowledge captured before people leave. The departures that used to surprise leadership now trigger strategic responses.

Three capabilities, one pattern: AI removes friction from the entire joiner-mover-leaver lifecycle. Not by replacing humans, but by collapsing the administrative overhead that has buried the actual talent work.

2. Your Pre-Summit Homework

Three things to bring to the session. Not to present, but to think about. Write them down. Short answers are fine. What matters is that you’ve actually wrestled with them.

1. A bold vision for 1 January 2027

If you fast-forwarded 20 months, what does your HR function look like? Not incremental. Drastically different. What’s disappeared? What’s new? What does “talent leadership” even mean? Write the headline version: if someone wrote a profile of your function in January 2027, what would it say?

2. The case for transformation

Why does your HR function need to transform, and why now? Not “AI is important” generically. The specific commercial, competitive, or structural reason transformation is non-negotiable for you. What breaks if you stay as you are? If you can’t articulate this in two sentences, your team won’t follow you.

3. The 2-3 KPIs that matter in the new era

What are the 2-3 metrics that tell you HR is winning in an AI-native world? Time-to-hire? Quality-of-hire? Internal mobility rate? Manager effectiveness score? Engagement-weighted retention? The old KPIs of cost-per-hire and headcount measure input. What measures outcome in a world where transactional work is automated?

3. Directional Nudges · Sense / Decide / Execute

Sense: From Manual Screening to Continuous Matching

What if every open role was automatically matched against 1.6 billion career profiles · filtered not by job titles but by actual skills?

Eightfold has indexed 1.6 billion career profiles and learned to match on capability, not credentials. A role requiring event-driven architecture, system design, and Go expertise becomes a query against a global skills graph · instantly surfacing internal candidates nobody considered and external talent you didn’t know existed. The job description · that fiction written to describe the last person in the role · becomes obsolete.

The structural change: screening stops being a recruiter task and becomes a matching algorithm. Recruiters shift from CV reader to relationship builder. Time-to-fill collapses from 44 days to 15-20 days. Cost-per-hire drops from $3-5K to $800-1.2K. Onboarding shifts from checklist to orchestration · AI provisions 15+ systems in parallel, assigns mentors, sequences training, and tracks milestones through day 90.

Decide: From Reactive Moves to Frictionless Internal Talent Flow

What if every internal move · contract amendment, cost centre remapping, payroll adjustment, benefits re-enrollment, systems access · completed in under 48 hours with zero HR ops involvement?

Today, an approved internal move is an administrative nightmare. The move decision happens on Monday. Systems updates happen over four weeks. Payroll doesn’t change until next month. Contract language differs between entities. Employees stay in the old seat while paperwork crawls.

Gloat and Fuel50 expose the opportunities. AI agents handle the logistics. Automation workflows trigger the entire process from a single approval event. Cost centre pulls from the new role definition. Contracts auto-amend based on employment entity. Payroll adjusts within hours. Systems provisioning happens in parallel. The employee is fully operational before the move announcement.

The structural change: internal mobility becomes a core retention lever, not a side effect. Employees see pathways, match to roles by skills, and move fluidly. Tenure increases 40-50%. Recruitment costs drop because 60-70% of open roles get filled internally.

Execute: From Flight Risk Surprises to Predictive Intervention

What if your HR team knew 6 months ahead which employees were likely to leave · and what each intervention would take to keep them?

IBM’s model flags risk with 95% accuracy using 34+ HR signals: tenure trajectory, manager changes, compensation band, engagement scores, performance ratings. Three months before someone hands in notice, leadership already knows. The HRBP runs the intervention playbook: opportunity surfacing, compensation review, mentor assignment, stretch project.

Sensay captures institutional knowledge through structured AI interviews as people leave · making departing expertise searchable and accessible. The “irreplaceable” engineer’s context becomes documented before the exit.

The structural change: attrition moves from reactive problem to managed flow. Regrettable attrition drops 30-40% through early intervention. Knowledge loss becomes preventable.

The Loop Closes

In the AI-native HR model, sensing is continuous. Deciding is frictionless. Execution is automated. The talent lifecycle stops being a series of events and becomes a fluid flow. The HR function stops processing transactions and starts stewarding capability.

Thought Exercise

If AI handled 90% of transactional HR · recruitment screening, onboarding admin, internal move paperwork, exit processing, employee queries · what would your HR team actually do? What new work emerges? And what does your org chart look like in 18 months?

4. Tough Questions to Ask Yourself

Sense

  • How do you redesign recruitment to assume 90% of screening, scheduling, and initial engagement happens through AI without human friction?
  • If skills matching was continuous and automatic, would job descriptions disappear?
  • What changes about your employer brand when candidates experience 15-day hiring instead of 44?

Decide

  • If an internal move required zero HR ops · just an approval button triggering 48 hours of automation · would your employees move more?
  • What’s the difference between “we don’t have good internal mobility” and “internal mobility is too painful to attempt”?
  • If AI could flag compensation inequity before it drives attrition, would you have to adjust faster?

Execute

  • How do you measure HR impact if you’re not counting tickets processed or forms submitted?
  • If you could predict attrition 3-6 months ahead with 95% accuracy, what’s your retention intervention playbook?
  • How do you preserve institutional knowledge when departures are predictable but still happen?

North Star

  • If HR shrinks from 75 to 30-40 people (for a 7,000-person company), what are the 5 roles that remain?
  • Does your CHRO become a technology partner, a culture architect, a workforce strategist · or all three?
  • When HR is invisible and working, how do you demonstrate strategic value to the board?

6. Hands-On Exercises

Do at least one before the session. These put you in the shoes of an AI-native HR function · not an opinion about the future, but a working prototype.

Exercise 1: The Skills Matching Prototype  ·  30 min

Pick 3 open roles in your org. Write a detailed job description for each. Then, using Claude or another AI, generate a skills profile for each role · decomposing the role into 12-15 required capabilities. Next, take a team of 15 employees. Ask AI to analyse their LinkedIn profiles, internal project history, and performance reviews to build a capability profile for each. Match employees to roles by skills, not current position. How many matches would you never have considered?

Output: A visual skills-to-role matching analysis showing missed internal candidates.

Tools: Claude, LinkedIn data, internal HR data · Time: 30 min

Exercise 2: The Internal Move Workflow Automator  ·  60 min

Map every step triggered when someone changes roles internally: contract amendment, cost centre change, payroll adjustment, benefits update, reporting line change, systems access reconfiguration, announcement workflow. Time each step. Estimate total elapsed time. Then design an automation workflow that executes all steps from a single “move approved” trigger. Target: under 48 hours end-to-end.

Output: A working internal move automation workflow with before/after time comparison.

Tools: Make/Zapier, HRIS/payroll/benefits API, DocuSign API · Time: 60 min

Exercise 3: The Predictive Attrition Model  ·  90 min

Gather anonymised HR data for 50-100 recent leavers: tenure trajectory, manager changes, compensation band, engagement scores, performance ratings, peer departures. Feed this to Claude or Python-based ML. Ask AI to identify patterns and build a flight-risk model. Run the model against your current employee population. Compare predictions to what HRBPs intuitively know. Which signals matter most?

Output: A working flight-risk model with predictions and pattern analysis.

Tools: Claude, Python, anonymised HR data · Time: 90 min
