Future of IT

What if your IT team could focus on strategy instead of firefighting?

1. The Shift

Infrastructure operations are locked in a cycle that hasn't fundamentally changed in two decades: alerts arrive, humans investigate, teams escalate, and issues eventually resolve - often hours or days later. ServiceNow disclosed that its AI bot is now resolving 90% of its own internal help desk tickets, handling password resets, access requests, software provisioning, and VPN issues autonomously. Datadog reduced alert noise by 69% for a customer through intelligent correlation and enrichment. PagerDuty customers report MTTR reductions of 40-60% when they deploy AI-driven incident response automation. Moveworks achieves 80% first-contact resolution rates for service desk requests.

The pattern across these platforms is identical: AI shifts operations from reactive firefighting to predictive intervention. Where humans once managed thousands of alerts manually, AI now correlates signals, identifies root causes, and auto-remediates known issues before business impact. Where service desk agents spent 70% of their time on routine requests, AI now handles those autonomously - freeing humans for genuine problems.

The structural transformation isn't about replacing people. It's about moving them upstream: from "keeping the lights on" to "ensuring they stay on." The CIOs who win are the ones building the autonomous infrastructure layer while simultaneously rethinking what their teams actually do.

2. Your Pre-Summit Homework

Three things to bring to the session. Not to present, but to think about. Write them down. Short answers are fine. What matters is that you've actually wrestled with them.

1. A bold vision for 1 January 2027

If you fast-forwarded 8 months, what does your IT function look like? Not incremental. Drastically different. What's disappeared? What's new? What does an IT team even look like? Write the headline version: if someone wrote a profile of your IT function in January 2027, what would it say?

2. The case for transformation

Why does your IT function need to transform, and why now? Not "AI is important" generically. The specific operational, competitive, or structural reason transformation is non-negotiable for you. What breaks if you stay as you are? If you can't articulate this in two sentences, your team won't follow you.

3. The KPIs that matter in the new era

What are the 2-3 metrics that tell you IT is winning in an AI-native world? MTTR? Ticket deflection? Change failure rate? Employee-to-IT-staff ratio? Security incident dwell time? The old KPIs of alert volume and ticket count measure activity. What measures outcome in a world where routine issues are auto-resolved?

3. Directional Nudges - Sense / Decide / Execute

Sense: From Alert Overload to Intelligent Observability

What if your entire IT infrastructure was self-monitoring? Thousands of alerts arrive each day, but AI correlates them, suppresses the 70% that are noise, identifies root causes in the 30% that matter, and predicts failures before they impact users?

This is already production. Datadog Event Management consolidates alerts from across observability and third-party tools into one intelligent view, reducing alert incidents by up to 69%. Dynatrace's Davis AI engine auto-discovers infrastructure topology and identifies anomalies before they cascade. Wiz uses AI to scan cloud estates and distinguish real security risks from misconfigurations that require no action. CrowdStrike AI detects behavioral anomalies across endpoints and cloud identities in real-time.

The structural change: the monitoring function stops being dashboards that humans watch 24/7 and starts being an autonomous intelligence layer. On-call engineers shift from "responding to alerts" to "resolving what the system flagged as critical." The signal-to-noise ratio moves from 30-70 (useful-to-noise) toward 80-20, because AI stops treating every threshold breach as an emergency.

Decide: From Ticket Queues to Instant Resolutions

What if 80% of service desk requests - access, software, hardware, password, network - were resolved by AI without any human touching the ticket?

ServiceNow's own help desk shows this working at scale: 90% of L1 volume is handled autonomously, with resolution rates above 99% for those categories. Moveworks reports first-contact resolution rates of 80% when integrated with existing ITSM platforms. These systems understand context (role, department, permissions), execute the action (grant access, provision software, reset password), and confirm completion - all without human escalation.

The structural change: the service desk transforms from a cost center of high-volume, low-complexity work into a triage function for genuinely complex issues. New hire onboarding goes from "2-5 days for IT to provision access and software" to "completed in under an hour by AI." License management, security group assignments, and email provisioning happen autonomously on hire or role change.

Execute: From Incident Chaos to Autonomous Remediation

What if the infrastructure healed itself? 80% of incidents - service restarts, failover triggers, cache clears, resource scaling - were resolved automatically before humans were even alerted?

PagerDuty customers deploying auto-remediation see MTTR drop 40-60% because the system detects issues (restarted Apache, scaled database, cleared cache) and escalates pre-diagnosed context to L2/L3 engineers. The engineer doesn't spend 30 minutes investigating - the system's already done it. They spend 30 seconds deciding on next steps.

Incident response automation also changes post-mortems. Every incident leaves behind a decision trail: what was detected, what was tried, what worked, what was escalated. AI learns from this and expands the set of auto-remediable issues continuously.

The structural change: on-call rotations shift from 24/7 "alert response" to "complex problem resolution." The majority of incidents resolve without human involvement. The humans who remain in the on-call pool are cloud architects and infrastructure engineers debugging novel issues, not firefighters.

The Loop Closes

In the AI-native model, infrastructure monitoring becomes continuous and predictive. Response becomes autonomous for known issues. Execution feedback trains the next generation of detection and remediation. The IT ops function stops operating in "incident response cycles" and starts operating in "prevention and continuous improvement."

North-Star Target

From ~200 (Infrastructure, Security, Service Desk, IT Ops, Cloud Ops, Networking) to 80-120 people

IT staff per 1,000 employees: from 29 to 12-18

AI resolves 80%+ of service desk tickets at first contact. Infrastructure monitoring is autonomous. Incident response is 70%+ auto-remediated. Security monitoring shifts from alert triage to threat hunting. The survivors are infrastructure architects, security engineers, cost optimization specialists, and platform enablers.

5. Hands-On Exercises

Do at least one before the session. Do all four if you want to arrive with working prototypes.

Exercise 1: The AI Service Desk Prototype  ·  60 min

Export your top 100 IT tickets (anonymised). Build an AI agent using Claude that can resolve routine ones: access requests, software installs, password resets, VPN troubleshooting. Test it against 20 new tickets. Measure resolution rate and time vs. your current service desk SLA.

Output: A working AI IT service desk prototype with measured performance vs. human baseline.

Tools: Claude API, ticket data, Python/HTML

Exercise 2: The Alert Correlation & Remediation Report  ·  90 min

Pull a week of monitoring alerts from your infrastructure. Use AI to identify patterns: which alerts correlate (cause-and-effect), which are redundant, which could be auto-remediated. Build a dashboard showing: alert reduction potential, auto-remediation opportunities, MTTR improvement.

Output: An interactive analysis showing alert noise reduction and auto-remediation opportunities.

Tools: Python, Claude, alert data, HTML/ECharts

Exercise 3: The IT Provisioning Automation Workflow  ·  90 min

Build an automated new-hire IT provisioning workflow. Trigger: a new hire record in your HRIS. Actions: create email account, provision access to standard tools, assign security groups based on role, configure VPN, notify manager. Deploy using Make/Zapier or Python.

Output: A working auto-provisioning workflow that sets up a new employee’s IT environment in minutes instead of days.

Tools: Make/Zapier, HRIS/API integrations, Okta/Google Workspace/AD

Exercise 4: Rebuild Your Incident Retro as an AI-Generated Artefact  ·  60-90 min

Stop writing your incident retros as static markdown pages. Rebuild one as a live, interactive artefact. Pull your last 20 incidents: detection time, MTTR, root cause, auto-remediation potential, escalation path. Use Claude to build an artefact you can click into: timeline visualization, auto-remediation opportunities, trend analysis, and what-if scenarios. Run your next retro from the artefact.

Output: An interactive incident analysis artefact that replaces static retro documentation.

Tools: Claude, Claude Artifacts, incident data, JSON export
